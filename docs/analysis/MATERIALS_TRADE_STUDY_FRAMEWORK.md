# Materials Trade Study Framework

IX-GCR-SPE evaluates materials by role, not by hype.

## 1. Evaluation dimensions

Each candidate material or material family is scored qualitatively against:

- hydrogen content / low-Z suitability
- secondary-particle behavior
- areal-density efficiency
- structural usefulness
- thermal burden
- containment burden
- manufacturability
- inspectability
- compatibility with shield health monitoring
- survivability at seams and hatches

## 2. Baseline material families

### 2.1 Water / water-equivalent media
**Role:** primary local SPE shelter mass, chamber fill, reconfigurable protection.

**Why it stays strong:**
- hydrogen-rich,
- widely understood,
- supports crew-consumables integration,
- can be rearranged around occupied zones.

**Weaknesses:**
- leakage,
- slosh / dynamic management,
- freezing / thermal issues,
- dead mass if poorly placed.

### 2.2 Polyethylene / UHMWPE-family materials
**Role:** structural low-Z shield layers, liners, hatch covers, internal local shielding.

**Why it stays strong:**
- proven low-Z baseline,
- simpler than cryogenic branches,
- useful at crew-side boundaries.

**Weaknesses:**
- mass still adds up quickly,
- not a complete GCR solution,
- may need structural pairing.

### 2.3 Hydrogen-boron-nitrogen composite branches
**Role:** graded secondary management and multifunctional structural branches.

**Why it stays strong:**
- targets low-Z shielding plus neutron-conscious behavior,
- structurally more interesting than simple polymer blocks.

**Weaknesses:**
- maturity and manufacturability vary,
- performance must be modeled for exact composition.

### 2.4 Cryogenic hydrogen
**Role:** extreme-performance passive branch for annular vault shielding.

**Why it is attractive:**
- highest-value hydrogen-per-mass direction,
- serious path to large areal density around a small vault.

**Why it is painful:**
- cryogenic storage,
- thermal isolation,
- boiloff and containment,
- hatch-path integration,
- maintenance and safety burden.

### 2.5 Hydride modules, especially lithium-hydride branch
**Role:** aggressive local vault enhancement where hydrogen density matters.

**Why it is attractive:**
- public literature shows strong shielding promise in some trade studies,
- can be modularized for a small protected zone.

**Weaknesses:**
- handling and encapsulation concerns,
- chemical compatibility burden,
- thermal and containment details matter.

## 3. Materials that are demoted, not deleted

### 3.1 Aluminum and higher-Z structural defaults
Still relevant structurally, but not treated as the primary crew-shield answer.

### 3.2 Diamond
Diamond is retained for:
- detectors,
- dosimetry,
- harsh-environment sensing,
- specialty wear surfaces.

Diamond is demoted as a bulk shield concept because multilayer crystal stacks do
not bypass the core realities of areal density, broad incident angles, and
secondary-particle production.

## 4. Configuration philosophy

The best-performing architecture is not expected to be one material.
It is expected to be a **graded stack**:

1. outer survivability layer  
2. hydrogen-rich bulk mass  
3. boron/nitrogen-aware cleanup layer  
4. inner low-Z liner  
5. monitored hatch and penetration treatment  

## 5. Decision rule

A candidate moves forward only if it improves at least one of the following
without creating a larger hidden penalty elsewhere:

- local SPE survivability,
- local GCR reduction,
- weak-zone integrity,
- mass-use efficiency around the vault,
- detectability of degraded protection state.
